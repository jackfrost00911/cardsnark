Student Credit Cards & Free Pizza: What to Know Before You Sign Up

Because trading your financial future for a $2 slice of pepperoni is a bad deal.

6 min read • Updated Nov 2025

It’s the first week of the semester. You’re walking through the quad, trying to find a building that doesn't exist on the map, when you see it: A table. A clipboard. And a stack of warm, greasy pizza boxes.

A friendly recruiter waves you over. "Hey! Want a free slice? Just fill out this form for a credit card! You get a free T-shirt too!"

Stop. Drop the pizza. Walk away.

That recruiter does not care about your credit score. They get paid a commission for every warm body they sign up. If you sign that form without reading the fine print, you aren't getting "free lunch"—you are potentially signing up for a predatory nightmare that will haunt your credit report until you're 30.

The "Student Card" Advantage (Use It While You Can)

Here is the irony: While some student cards are scams, the good student cards are actually better than the cards your parents have.

Banks are desperate to hook you young. They know if you get their card now, you’ll probably keep it forever because changing banks is "too much effort." To bribe you, they lower their standards. They don't require a massive credit history. They just need to know you are enrolled in school.

You have a 4-year window to get approved for cards that would normally reject you. Don't waste this superpower on a card with a picture of a bulldog on it.

Snark Tip: If a "Pizza Plug" or someone on Instagram asks for your card details to get you "discounted food," that is not a student discount. That is a felony. It's called carding, and it involves stolen credit cards. Don't go to jail for Chipotle.

The Top 3 Cards That Actually Respect You

Ignore the table in the quad. These are the cards you should actually apply for online.

1. Discover it® Student Cash Back

This is the king of student cards. Why? Because they match all your cash back at the end of the first year.

  • The Rewards: 5% cash back on rotating categories (like Amazon, Restaurants, Grocery Stores) on up to $1,500 in purchases each quarter.
  • The Math: If you earn $150 in cash back your first year, they give you another $150. Boom. $300 for doing nothing.
  • The Requirement: No credit score needed to apply. Just proof you're a student.

2. Capital One Savor Student

If you refuse to track "rotating categories" because you have enough homework already, get this one. It is designed for the college lifestyle: eating and streaming.

  • The Rewards: Unlimited 3% cash back on dining, entertainment, popular streaming services, and at grocery stores.
  • Why it wins: It covers late-night tacos, your Spotify subscription, and the groceries you swear you're going to cook but eventually let rot in the fridge.
  • Foreign Fees: None. Taking a semester abroad? This card works everywhere without charging you extra.

3. Chase Freedom Rise®

This is the new kid on the block. It's a "starter" card that doesn't strictly require you to be a student, but it's perfect if you are one.

  • The Trick: Approval is much easier if you have a Chase checking account with at least $250 in it before you apply.
  • The Rewards: A flat 1.5% cash back on everything. No categories to remember.
  • The Path: It puts you in the Chase ecosystem early, making it easier to get the premium cards (like the Sapphire Preferred) once you graduate and get a real job.

The "Predator" List (Run Away)

If you see these names, hiss at them like a feral cat. These cards are "fee harvesters" designed to exploit people who don't know better.

  • Credit One: (Not Capital One). They often charge annual fees billed monthly. Imagine paying rent for your credit card.
  • First Premier / Premier Bankcard: They have been known to charge "processing fees" just to open the account. You could pay $170 in fees just to get a $300 limit.
  • Any card that doesn't report to all 3 bureaus: If it doesn't build your credit score, it's not a credit card. It's a gift card with fees.

The "Golden Rules" for Students

1. Never carry a balance.
The interest rate on student cards is often 25% - 30%. If you buy a $1,000 laptop and pay the minimum, that laptop will cost you $2,000 by the time you pay it off. Don't be a statistic.

2. Keep your "Utilization" low.
Student cards have low limits (usually $500 - $1,000). If you max it out, your credit score will tank. Try to keep your balance below 30% of your limit (so, under $150 on a $500 card).

3. Don't close it when you graduate.
The "Age of Credit History" is a huge factor in your score. Keep your first student card open forever (as long as it has no annual fee). Buy a pack of gum on it once a year to keep it active.
